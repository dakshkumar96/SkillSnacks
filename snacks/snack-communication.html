Effective Communication

Category: Communication • Estimated time: 8–10 minutes

Short, practical techniques to make your messages clearer and your listening more effective — includes templates, role-play exercises, and quick repair phrases for when things go wrong.

Intro

Good communication isn’t about fancy language — it’s about structure, clarity and being understood. This lesson focuses on a compact message structure, active listening micro-skills, and short practice drills you can do now.

What you'll learn

  • The 3-part message structure (Context → Point → Next step) you can use anywhere.
  • Two active listening techniques to use in conversations right away.
  • How to fix common communication breakdowns with short repair phrases.

The core idea

People remember short, clear messages. Start with context, say the point, finish with a clear next step. Follow that with listening: paraphrase and ask one clarifying question. This reduces misunderstandings and speeds decisions.

Action plan — use this structure

1 — The 3-part message (use in 30 seconds)

Structure your spoken or written message like this:

  1. Context — one short sentence explaining why you’re speaking. (e.g., “About the monthly report…”)
  2. Point — the main idea or request, one sentence. (e.g., “I need your data by Tuesday.”)
  3. Next step — what you want them to do and when. (e.g., “Can you send the spreadsheet by Tuesday noon?”)

2 — Active listening (two micro-skills)

When someone speaks, use these two short habits:

  • Paraphrase: Repeat their key sentence in your own words. (“So you’re saying X…”)
  • Clarify: Ask one open question if something is unclear. (“What do you mean by…?” or “Which deadline do you mean?”)

3 — Repair phrases (use when things go wrong)

Short phrases to fix misunderstanding quickly:

  • “Sorry, I didn’t catch the last part — could you repeat that?”
  • “I think I understood X; did you mean Y?”
  • “Let me summarise to make sure we’re aligned…”

Examples & quick templates

Email template (work)

Subject: Action needed — Sales figures for Q2 (due Tue)

Hi [Name],

Context: I'm preparing the Q2 summary and need the sales figures for regions A and B.
Point: Could you send the data table with totals?
Next step: Please send the spreadsheet by Tuesday 12:00 so I can include it in the report.

Thanks,
[You]

Quick spoken example (30s)

Context: “On the design review…” Point: “I think the header is too large for mobile.” Next step: “Can you test 3 smaller sizes and share screenshots by tomorrow?”

Role-play exercises (practice)

Exercise A — 1-minute message

Task: Pick a short request you need to make (e.g., ask for a file). Write the Context → Point → Next step in 1 minute, then practise saying it out loud once.

Exercise B — Listening check (2 people, 3 minutes)

  1. Person A speaks for 60 seconds about a small problem they had today.
  2. Person B paraphrases (one sentence) and asks one clarifying question.
  3. Switch roles.

Exercise C — Repair drill (1 minute)

Practice the repair phrases: intentionally mumble one line, then practise responding with “Sorry, can you repeat that?” and summarising back.

Common mistakes & quick fixes

  • Over-explaining: Too much context buries the point. Fix: Lead with the point, then add the context if needed.
  • Ambiguous requests: Without a deadline or next step, people delay. Fix: Always include what and when.
  • Passive listening: Nodding isn't enough — paraphrase to confirm understanding.

Advanced tips (when you have more time)

  • Use the “if/then” format for conditional requests: “If you can't do X by Tuesday, then please propose an alternative date.”
  • For difficult feedback, lead with a factual observation, then the impact, then a suggestion (e.g., “When X happened, Y happened; next time try Z”).
  • In group settings, name the next step and assign an owner to avoid diffusion of responsibility.

Short transcript / notes

Use Context → Point → Next step for clear messages. Pair that with paraphrase + clarification when listening. When misunderstandings occur, use concise repair phrases and re-state the desired action and deadline.
